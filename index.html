Preamble

The Pattern → Mechanism → Law Engine

Thermodynamics began not with equations but with observations that refused to be ignored. Water boils. Iron sinks — except when you shape it into a bowl. Rails buckle in summer. Heat always flows downhill. Every week in this course follows the same engine: we observe a pattern, propose a mechanism that explains it, then distil the mechanism into a law — a compact description we can use to predict what happens next.

Along the way, we'll build two parallel vocabularies. The macroscopic one speaks of pressure, volume, temperature — quantities you can read off a gauge. The microscopic one speaks of molecules bouncing, colliding, and sharing energy. The deep insight of thermodynamics is that these two descriptions are the same system viewed at different scales. That's the reductionist paradigm, and it is among the most powerful ideas in all of science.

We start where your intuition already lives — things that float and sink — and end with the zeroth and first laws of thermodynamics, the gas laws, Maxwell's Demon, and Joule's waterfall. By the end, you will understand why a perfect engine is impossible, why your coffee always cools, and what Brownian motion tells us about atoms nobody could yet see.

Week 01 · Lessons 1 – 4

Density: The Pivot Between Mass and Volume

"Which is heavier — a kilogram of feathers or a kilogram of steel?"

We begin with the most common misconception in physics: confusing mass with density. Students handle real objects, weigh them, measure their volumes, and discover that density is the intensive variable that links the extensive quantities of mass and volume. The week ends with the puzzle that sets up buoyancy: why does shape determine whether metal floats?

Lesson 1 · 45 min Mass ≠ Weight ≠ Density

Hook: Show the six images from Slide 1 of the presentation. Ask: "What concept connects a toilet, Brownian motion, a crystal lattice, buoyancy, a ball on a surface, and a manometer?" Let students discuss freely for 5 minutes.

Hands-on · 15 min

Solids: Students measure dimensions of regular solids (steel, aluminium, wood, foam cubes) with vernier callipers, weigh on a digital balance, calculate ρ = m/V. Rank the cubes. For irregular solids, use a eureka (displacement) can: lower the object in, catch the overflow in a measuring cylinder, read Vdisplaced.

Liquids: Weigh an empty calorimeter. Fill with a known volume of liquid (water, saltwater, cooking oil). Reweigh. Calculate average density of each fluid. Compare: why is saltwater denser than freshwater?

Materials: Vernier callipers, density cubes, irregular solids, eureka can, measuring cylinders, calorimeters, digital balance, water, saltwater, cooking oil, calculator

Key distinction: Mass is intrinsic (scalar, kg). Weight is gravitational force (vector, N). Density = m/V is what connects them to size. Walk through the Weight–Density–Mass infographic (Slide 2).

ρ = m / V     W = m × g
ρ in kg/m³ · W in N · g ≈ 9.81 m/s²
experiment discussion definitions
Teacher notes
Push students on: "Would these cubes have the same mass on the Moon?" (Yes.) "The same weight?" (No.) "The same density?" (Yes — density is intrinsic to the material, not the gravitational field.) This sets up the Earth-vs-Moon puzzle in Lesson 2.
Lesson 2 · 45 min Earth vs Moon: The Invariance of Mass

Starter: Present the aluminium-vs-lead puzzle (Slides 3–4). "Your mother gives you 1 kg of aluminium and 1 kg of lead. Which is more massive on the Moon?"

Students work through the four sub-questions in pairs: (a) same mass on Earth, (b) same mass on Moon, (c) different apparent weight on Earth (atmospheric buoyancy!), (d) same weight on Moon (no atmosphere).

Practical · 15 min

In water, not air: Hang a 1 kg aluminium block and a 1 kg lead block from spring balances (or Sparkvue force sensors). Read their weights in air — identical. Now lower both into a beaker of water. The aluminium block shows a larger reduction in apparent weight because it has a bigger volume and displaces more water. The difference is clearly visible — no need for a 0.01 g balance.

Materials: 1 kg aluminium block, 1 kg lead block, 2 × spring balances or Sparkvue force sensors, large beaker of water, retort stands + clamps

Exit ticket: "Explain in two sentences why mass is invariant but weight is not."

Lesson 3 · 45 min The Oil Drop Experiment: Measuring the Invisible

Big idea: If you know two of the three quantities — volume, area, height — you can calculate the third. An oil monolayer is one molecule thick. By measuring the area it spreads to, and knowing the volume of the drop, you can estimate molecular size. This is density thinking applied at the nanoscale.

Full Practical · 30 min

Method: Dust the surface of a large tray of water with lycopodium powder. Using a micropipette, deposit a single small drop of oleic acid (dissolved in ethanol at known concentration, e.g. 1:500). The ethanol evaporates; the oil spreads into a roughly circular monolayer, pushing the lycopodium aside. Measure the diameter of the clear circle with a ruler. Repeat 3–5 times.

Calculate:

• Volume of oil in one drop: V = (volume of micropipette drop) × (concentration of oleic acid)
• Area of circle: A = πr² (measure diameter, halve, square, multiply by π)
• Height of monolayer: h = V/A
• This h ≈ 1 × 10⁻⁹ m — roughly the length of one oleic acid molecule!

Error analysis: The circle is not perfectly round — measure diameter in several directions and average. The micropipette delivers variable drop sizes — calibrate by counting drops per known volume. Discuss systematic vs random errors.

Materials: Large shallow tray, water, lycopodium powder, micropipette, oleic acid solution (in ethanol, ~0.05% v/v), ruler, calculator

Connection to density: If you know the molecular height and the density of oleic acid (ρ ≈ 895 kg/m³), you can estimate the mass of a single molecule. This is the same ρ = m/V logic, now applied at molecular scale — a beautiful preview of the micro→macro bridge we'll formalise in Week 8.

practical molecular size error analysis
Teacher notes
This experiment has a rich history — it was first done by Lord Rayleigh and Agnes Pockels in the 1890s. It's one of the earliest estimates of molecular dimensions. Students often find it surprising that such a simple setup yields a nanometre-scale measurement. The error analysis is genuinely instructive: the dominant uncertainty is usually in the drop volume, not the circle diameter. Encourage students to think about which measurement limits their precision.
Lesson 4 · 45 min Density ↔ Pressure: Two Faces of Compactness

Bridging lesson. Present the Density–Pressure comparison diagram (Slide 7). Both are intensive variables. Both describe "compactness" — density is mass per volume, pressure is force per area. Both use the word per: they are ratios.

Quick Demo · 5 min

Stand on soft ground in normal shoes, then in stilettos (or put a brick on a nail vs flat). Same weight, different pressure. Same mass, different density (compressed foam vs regular foam).

Materials: Foam block, heavy book, sharp pencil (blunt end vs point)

P = F / A     Pa = N/m² = kg/(m·s²)
Density: kg/m³ (mass per volume) · Pressure: N/m² (force per area)

Homework: "Why is a knife sharp? Answer in terms of pressure."

Week 02 · Lessons 5 – 8

Pressure: From Molecular Collisions to Atmospheric Gradients

"Why does pressure increase with depth in water but decrease with altitude in air?"

Having introduced pressure as force-per-area, we now ask: what causes pressure? The answer is molecular collisions — the mechanism behind the pattern. We then explore how pressure varies in fluids, discovering that water (incompressible) and air (compressible) create fundamentally different pressure profiles.

Lesson 5 · 45 min Pressure as Molecular Bombardment

Starter: Show the gas-in-a-cylinder diagram (Slide 10) — molecules bouncing off walls. Each collision transfers momentum → force → force per area = pressure.

Simulation · 10 min

Use PhET Gas Properties simulation. Students vary number of particles, volume, and temperature. Observe pressure changes in real time. Identify qualitative patterns before formalising.

Materials: Laptops/tablets, PhET "Gas Properties" sim

Key idea: Pressure is not something a gas "has" passively — it arises from the process of billions of collisions per second. This is the microscopic mechanism for a macroscopic measurement.

P = F/A = (momentum transfer per collision × collision rate) / area
Qualitative at this stage — we formalise in Week 7
simulation mechanism
Lesson 6 · 45 min Atmospheric Pressure & Altitude

Hook: "If you carried a barometer up Everest, what would it read?" Show Slide 14 — the exponential decay of atmospheric pressure with altitude. Gravity compresses the atmosphere; more air above you = more weight pushing down = more pressure.

Demo · 10 min

Crushing can: Heat a small amount of water in an aluminium can. Invert into cold water. The can crushes — atmospheric pressure wins when internal pressure drops.

Materials: Empty aluminium drinks can, Bunsen burner, trough of cold water, tongs

Discuss the simulation from Slide 14: particles concentrated at the bottom of a tall box (gravity pulls them down), rarefied at top. Pressure = weight of air column above. This is a concentration gradient.

demo gradient exponential decay
Lesson 7 · 45 min Hydrostatic Pressure: Linear vs Exponential

Key contrast: Pressure increases linearly with depth in water but exponentially decreases with altitude in air (Slide 15). Why? Water is essentially incompressible — its density is constant. Air is compressible — its density varies with height.

Practical · 20 min

Use a tall plastic tube (2 m) filled with water. Attach a pressure sensor (or a simple U-tube manometer) at different depths. Record pressure vs depth. Plot the graph — it should be linear.

Materials: Tall clear tube, rubber bungs with holes, manometer/pressure sensor, ruler, water

P = ρgh + Patm
Linear in h because ρwater ≈ constant

Discussion: "If water were compressible like air, what would the pressure-depth graph look like?" (Exponential — more compressed water at the bottom would have higher density, creating even more pressure.)

practical compressibility
Lesson 8 · 45 min Manometers, Barometers & Terminal Velocity

Instruments: Introduce the manometer (Slide 11) as a device that measures pressure difference using the height of a liquid column. The barometer uses the same principle against a vacuum.

Build-it · 15 min

Build a simple U-tube manometer from clear plastic tubing and coloured water. Connect one end to a sealed syringe. Push the syringe — observe the fluid level change. This is gauge pressure in action.

Materials: Clear flexible tubing, food colouring, water, syringe, ruler, retort stand

Connection to drag: The raindrop MCQ (Slide 16). At terminal velocity, air resistance (a pressure phenomenon) exactly balances weight. Fdrag = W. No net force → no acceleration → constant velocity. This links pressure back to Newtonian mechanics.

Quiz: 3-question mini-assessment on pressure concepts covered so far.

Week 03 · Lessons 9 – 12

Buoyancy: Why Shape Defeats Density

"How can a steel ship float when a steel ball sinks?"

Archimedes' principle unifies everything so far: density, pressure, and weight all meet in the buoyant force. The key insight is that displacement — the volume of fluid pushed aside — determines whether an object floats. This is why shape matters: a flat bowl displaces more water per kilogram of iron than a compact sphere.

Lesson 9 · 45 min Archimedes' Principle

Demo: The floating-vs-sinking bowl (Slides 12–13). Show two aluminium foil "boats" — one flat, one crushed into a ball. Same mass, same material, same density of aluminium. But the flat one floats and the ball sinks. Why?

Hands-on · 20 min

Students make tinfoil boats and test how many pennies they can hold before sinking. Competition: whose boat holds the most? After, discuss: successful designs maximise displacement volume relative to weight.

Materials: Aluminium foil sheets (same size for all), pennies/weights, water trough

Fb = ρfluid × Vdisplaced × g
Buoyant force = weight of displaced fluid

Core logic: Object floats when Fb ≥ W. Since Fb depends on Vdisplaced, and W depends on mass, the trick is to maximise the envelope volume (shape) while keeping the material mass fixed.

competition demo Archimedes
Lesson 10 · 45 min Floating, Sinking & Neutral Buoyancy

Three regimes: ρobject < ρfluid → floats. ρobject > ρfluid → sinks. ρobject = ρfluid → neutrally buoyant (submarines, fish).

Practical · 20 min

Cartesian diver: Build a Cartesian diver from a plastic bottle, a small inverted test tube (or sauce packet), and water. Squeeze the bottle → diver sinks. Release → diver rises. The mechanism: squeezing compresses the air pocket, reducing volume, increasing average density.

Materials: 2 L plastic bottles, small sauce packets or inverted pen caps with Blu-Tack, water

Quantitative problem: "An ice cube of mass 50 g floats in water. What volume is submerged?" (Use ρice = 917 kg/m³, ρwater = 1000 kg/m³.)

Cartesian diver calculations
Lesson 11 · 45 min The Buoyancy of Air: Revisiting Aluminium vs Lead

Return to the Slide 5 puzzle: on Earth, 1 kg of aluminium actually weighs less than 1 kg of lead on a sensitive scale. The atmosphere exerts a buoyant force on both, but the aluminium (being less dense) has a larger volume and thus displaces more air.

Calculation · 15 min

Calculate the buoyant force of air on 1 kg of aluminium (ρ = 2700 kg/m³) vs 1 kg of lead (ρ = 11340 kg/m³). Use ρair = 1.2 kg/m³. The difference is ~0.3 grams-force — detectable on a good balance!

Materials: Calculator, worked example sheet

Extension: This is why precision balances in metrology labs operate in vacuum chambers. Every measurement is a buoyancy problem.

calculation real-world
Lesson 12 · 45 min Review & Problem Set: Density–Pressure–Buoyancy

Consolidation lesson. Structured problem set covering all three weeks. Mix of qualitative (explain why...) and quantitative (calculate...) questions.

Sample problems:

1. A block of wood (ρ = 600 kg/m³) is placed in oil (ρ = 800 kg/m³). What fraction is submerged?
2. Explain why a helium balloon rises using the concept of buoyancy.
3. A diver is at 30 m depth in seawater (ρ = 1025 kg/m³). Calculate the total pressure.
4. Why does a hot air balloon float? (Links to next week: temperature affects density.)

Peer marking with model answers. Identify common errors.

Week 04 · Lessons 13 – 16

Temperature: What Expansion Reveals

"How do we use one physical change to measure another?"

Temperature is our first truly abstract thermodynamic concept: you can't touch it directly, only infer it from something that changes — a column of mercury, the length of a metal bar, the pressure in a sealed gas. These are thermometric variables. The idea that we measure the invisible through the visible is the philosophical heart of experimental physics.

Lesson 13 · 45 min What is Temperature? Kelvin & Celsius

Starter: "Is temperature a thing or a measurement?" Discussion. Temperature is a state variable — it describes the system's internal energy, not a substance that flows. (Heat is what flows.)

Introduce the Kelvin scale (Slide 19). Key facts: absolute zero = 0 K = −273.15°C. A change of 1 K = a change of 1°C. Kelvin has no negative values — it's a true ratio scale.

T(K) = θ(°C) + 273.15
Absolute zero: all molecular motion at minimum (not zero — quantum mechanics!)

Fixed-points puzzle (Slide 20): A thermometer reads −10°C to 110°C. What are the lower and upper fixed points? (0°C and 100°C — the range of the scale differs from the range of the thermometer.)

definitions philosophy
Lesson 14 · 45 min Linear Thermal Expansion

Hook: Photo of buckled railway tracks (Slide 21). Why? Metals expand when heated. The fractional change in length is proportional to the temperature change.

Practical · 25 min

Heat a metal rod (e.g., brass or aluminium) clamped at one end using steam. Measure the expansion using a dial gauge or micrometer at the free end. Record ΔL for measured ΔT. Calculate α.

Materials: Metal expansion apparatus, steam generator, dial gauge/micrometer, thermometer, metre ruler

ΔL / L₀ = α · ΔT
α = coefficient of linear thermal expansion (K⁻¹)

Refer to the table of expansion coefficients (Slide 24): aluminium (24 × 10⁻⁶), steel (13 × 10⁻⁶), fused quartz (0.59 × 10⁻⁶). Why is Pyrex used for lab glassware? (Low α → doesn't crack with temperature changes.)

practical expansion
Lesson 15 · 45 min Thermometric Variables: Expansion as Measurement

Big idea: A thermometer works because some measurable property varies linearly (or at least monotonically) with temperature. Show Slide 23: height of mercury column vs temperature — a straight line H = mT + c.

Three thermometer types (Slide 25): (1) Length of mercury column, (2) Volume of gas at constant pressure, (3) Pressure of gas at constant volume (CVGT).

Build-it · 20 min

Build a simple air thermometer: a flask of air connected by tubing to a column of coloured water. Heat the flask gently. The water level changes as the air expands. Calibrate two points (ice water = 0°C, hand warmth ≈ 37°C). Predict intermediate temperatures.

Materials: Round-bottom flask, glass/plastic tubing, coloured water, beaker, ice, thermometer for calibration

Discussion: "Why should you delay the reading of a thermometer placed in a new system?" (Thermal equilibrium must be reached — the thermometer must reach the temperature of the system. This previews the Zeroth Law.)

build-it thermometric variables
Lesson 16 · 45 min Mercury Thermometer Calculation & Isobars

Worked example (Slide 24): Mercury thermometer, L = 109 mm at 20.0°C, α = 6.1 × 10⁻⁵ K⁻¹. Temperature increases to 30.5°C. Calculate ΔL.

ΔL = α × L₀ × ΔT = 6.1 × 10⁻⁵ × 109 × 10.5 = 0.070 mm. Tiny! This is why we need fine capillary tubes.

Scalar fields: Temperature as a scalar field (Slide 17). Show the global temperature anomaly map and the weather map with isobars. Temperature and pressure are both scalar fields — they assign a single number to each point in space. Lines of equal value (isotherms, isobars) are the contours of these fields.

Exit question: "A bimetallic strip bends when heated. Explain why, using the concept of different thermal expansion coefficients."

Week 05 · Lessons 17 – 20

Heat ≠ Temperature: Specific Heat Capacity

"A warm bath versus a white-hot spark — which has more thermal energy?"

The most dangerous confusion in thermodynamics is treating heat and temperature as synonyms. Temperature tells you how hot; heat tells you how much energy transfers. A bath at 40°C contains vastly more thermal energy than a spark at 3000°C. This week we quantify the distinction through specific heat capacity, and we meet Joule's extraordinary experiment connecting mechanical work to heat.

Lesson 17 · 45 min Temperature ≠ Heat: The Bath vs the Spark

Starter: Present the Temperature–Heat comparison (Slide 27). Temperature is intensive (state variable, measured in K, doesn't depend on how much stuff you have). Heat is extensive (energy flow, measured in J, depends on mass).

Thought experiment: "Which will burn you more — touching water at 60°C or touching a metal spoon at 60°C?" (Same temperature, but the metal conducts heat faster and has a different specific heat capacity.)

Demo · 10 min

Heat equal masses of water and cooking oil to the same temperature. Place a small cube of butter on each. The oil melts the butter faster — not because it's hotter (same T), but because oil has a lower specific heat capacity, so it transfers heat differently. Actually, reconsider: present it as a question. Same T, same mass — will they melt butter at the same rate?

Materials: Two beakers, water, cooking oil, thermometer, butter cubes, hotplate

ΔQ = m · c · Δθ
c = specific heat capacity (J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹) · ΔQ = energy transferred · Δθ = temperature change
demo intensive vs extensive
Lesson 18 · 45 min Measuring Specific Heat Capacity: Solids

Full practical (Slide 31). Use electrical heating to measure the SHC of a metal block.

Practical · 35 min

Insert a heater and thermometer into holes in a metal block (aluminium or copper). Insulate. Apply known electrical power (P = IV) for time Δt. Record temperature rise Δθ. Calculate: c = PΔt / (m × Δθ).

Materials: Metal block with holes, immersion heater, thermometer, ammeter, voltmeter, power supply, stopwatch, insulation (e.g. cotton wool), digital balance

Error discussion: Why is the measured value always higher than the true value? (Heat loss to surroundings means some electrical energy doesn't go into heating the block, so you overestimate the energy needed per degree.)

practical SHC
Lesson 19 · 45 min Measuring SHC: Liquids & Comparing

Practical: Same method, but for water in a calorimeter (Slide 32). Additional considerations: the calorimeter itself absorbs heat, so we need to account for it. Stirring ensures uniform temperature.

Practical · 25 min

Use a calorimeter (polystyrene cup works fine) with known mass of water. Heat electrically. Calculate c for water. Compare to the known value: 4200 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹ — one of the highest of any common substance.

Materials: Polystyrene cup (calorimeter), stirrer, thermometer, immersion heater, ammeter, voltmeter, power supply, stopwatch, balance

Why water is special: High SHC makes water excellent for heating systems, cooking, and climate regulation. Coastal cities have milder weather because the ocean absorbs huge amounts of energy for small temperature changes.

practical real-world
Lesson 20 · 45 min Joule's Experiment: Mechanical Work → Heat

The historical experiment (Slide 34). Joule measured the temperature difference between water at the top and bottom of a 400 m waterfall. PE lost = mgΔh = m × 10 × 400 = 4000m J. Heat gained = mcΔθ. Set them equal: ΔT = 4000/4200 ≈ 0.95°C.

This was revolutionary: it proved that mechanical energy and heat are the same thing — different forms of energy, interconvertible. This is the essence of the First Law of Thermodynamics.

Mini-experiment · 15 min

Friction heating: Rub a metal rod vigorously with sandpaper for 2 minutes. Measure temperature before and after. Estimate the mechanical work done (force × distance of rubbing). Compare to mcΔθ. The numbers won't match perfectly — where did the "missing" energy go?

Materials: Copper rod, sandpaper, thermometer (or thermal camera if available), stopwatch

Key equation chain: Wmechanical = ΔEkinetic → ΔQheat = mcΔθ. Energy is conserved; it transforms between forms.

mini-experiment energy conservation history of science
Week 06 · Lessons 21 – 24

Efficiency: Why "Lost" Energy is Never Really Lost

"Where does the missing energy go — and why can't we get it back?"

Every machine transforms energy from one form to another, but some always ends up as heat — the "lowest quality" form of energy. This week connects the mechanical work from Week 5 to electrical energy, introduces efficiency, and builds toward the insight that energy degradation is inevitable. We also contrast evaporation with boiling — two mechanisms for the same phase change.

Lesson 21 · 45 min Electrical Input → Mechanical Output

Framework: Slide 35. Electrical energy in (Win = IVΔt), mechanical work out (Wout = mgh or Fd), and the difference = energy degraded to heat and sound.

Practical · 25 min

Use a small electric motor to lift a known mass through a measured height. Measure I and V during operation, time the lift. Calculate Win = IVΔt, Wout = mgh, and efficiency = Wout/Win.

Materials: Small DC motor, pulley, string, masses, ammeter, voltmeter, power supply, stopwatch, ruler

Efficiency = Wout / Win = useful energy / total energy input
Always < 1 (or < 100%) for real machines
practical efficiency
Lesson 22 · 45 min Energy Degradation: Why Heat is "Bad" Energy

Key insight: Heat is energy spread out over many randomly-moving particles. It's hard to collect back into organised motion. This is why efficiency < 100%: some input energy always degrades into disordered thermal energy.

Slide 33 — Work vs Impulse comparison. Work transfers energy via force over distance (scalar). Impulse transfers momentum via force over time (vector). Heat is the "hidden" form of energy — mechanical energy that has been redistributed into random molecular motion.

Demo · 10 min

Bouncing ball: Drop a ball from 1 m. Measure the rebound height. Each bounce converts some kinetic energy to thermal energy (and sound). Plot rebound height vs bounce number. The "lost" height = energy degraded to heat.

Materials: Various balls (rubber, tennis, golf), metre ruler, camera for slow-mo if available

Discussion: "Is a ball that bounces to 90% of its drop height 'more efficient' than one that bounces to 50%? In what sense?"

demo energy quality
Lesson 23 · 45 min Evaporation vs Boiling

Phase change context (Slide 49). Both evaporation and boiling convert liquid to gas, but by different mechanisms. Evaporation is a surface phenomenon driven by the fastest molecules escaping — it occurs at any temperature. Boiling is a bulk phenomenon occurring when vapour pressure equals atmospheric pressure — it requires a specific temperature.

Practical · 15 min

Place drops of water, ethanol, and acetone on the back of students' hands. Which evaporates fastest? Which feels coldest? (Fastest evaporation = most heat removed from skin = coldest sensation.) This directly connects to the idea that the most energetic molecules escape, leaving the liquid cooler.

Materials: Dropper bottles of water, ethanol, and acetone (or nail polish remover)

Connection: Evaporation is a cooling mechanism. This is why sweating works, why wet clothes feel cold, and why your coffee cools even below room temperature on a dry day (briefly). Link back to energy: the escaping molecules carry above-average kinetic energy, so the average KE (i.e., temperature) of the remaining liquid drops.

practical phase change
Lesson 24 · 45 min Mid-Course Assessment

Written assessment covering Weeks 1–6: density, pressure, buoyancy, temperature, SHC, efficiency, energy transformations. Mix of:

• Multiple-choice (concept check, ~10 questions)

The extended response requires students to chain: hot air is less dense → balloon's average density < air density → buoyant force > weight → it rises. This synthesises Weeks 1–4.

Week 07 · Lessons 25 – 28

Gas Laws: Patterns in Pressure, Volume, and Temperature

"Why does a balloon shrink in the freezer?"

The gas laws are among the most beautiful examples of the pattern → mechanism → law engine. Each law is an empirical pattern observed by experimentalists (Boyle, Charles, Gay-Lussac). Together they combine into PV/T = constant — the ideal gas law. But the laws are descriptions, not explanations. The explanation comes from the molecular model, which we develop in parallel.

Lesson 25 · 45 min Boyle's Law: P ∝ 1/V at Constant T

Pattern first: Show Slide 44. For a fixed mass of gas at constant temperature, pressure and volume are inversely proportional. P₁V₁ = P₂V₂.

Practical · 25 min

Use a Boyle's Law apparatus (syringe connected to pressure gauge, or a sealed column of air with oil). Vary volume, record pressure. Plot P vs V (hyperbola) and P vs 1/V (straight line through origin).

Materials: Boyle's Law apparatus (or large syringe + pressure sensor), ruler, graph paper or laptop for plotting

PV = constant  (at constant T)
P ∝ 1/V → halve the volume, double the pressure

Mechanism (Slide 48): Same molecules in smaller space → hit walls more often → higher pressure. Temperature is constant → average speed unchanged → each collision transfers the same momentum, but collisions are more frequent.

practical Boyle's Law
Lesson 26 · 45 min Charles' Law: V ∝ T at Constant P

Pattern: Slide 42 & 46. At constant pressure, volume is proportional to absolute temperature. All gases, when extrapolated, reach V = 0 at −273.15°C — absolute zero.

Practical · 25 min

Seal a column of air with a drop of oil in a capillary tube. Immerse in water baths of different temperatures. Measure the length of the air column (∝ volume) at each temperature. Plot V vs T(°C). Extrapolate to find where V = 0.

Materials: Capillary tubes, oil, beakers, thermometer, water baths at various temperatures (ice, room, warm, hot), ruler

V/T = constant  (at constant P, T in Kelvin)
V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂

Mechanism: Higher temperature → molecules move faster → hit walls harder and more often → at constant pressure, the only way to compensate is to expand (more space between collisions = same collision rate per unit area).

practical Charles' Law
Lesson 27 · 45 min Pressure Law: P ∝ T at Constant V

Pattern: Slide 45. At constant volume, pressure is directly proportional to absolute temperature. P/T = constant.

Practical · 25 min

Use a sealed flask connected to a pressure sensor. Immerse in water baths at various temperatures. Record P vs T. Plot P vs T (K) — should pass through the origin (extrapolated) with a straight line.

Materials: Round-bottom flask with bung and tubing to pressure sensor, water baths, thermometer, data logger if available

P/T = constant  (at constant V, T in Kelvin)
P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂ · Isochore = constant volume line

Connection: This is why tyre pressures increase on a hot day (constant volume, temperature rises → pressure rises). It's also the basis of the constant-volume gas thermometer (Slide 25) — the most accurate thermometer type.

Synthesis: Combine Boyle's, Charles', and the Pressure Law into the ideal gas equation (Slide 47).

Micro → Macro: The Thermodynamic Laws

"Can you cheat the universe if you're clever enough?"

We've spent seven weeks observing patterns and proposing mechanisms. Now we step back and ask: what are the laws that govern all of this? The Zeroth Law defines temperature. The First Law says energy is conserved. And Maxwell's Demon asks: could a sufficiently clever being violate the Second Law? Lesson 29 · 45 min Brownian Motion: Seeing the Invisible

Evidence for atoms: Slides 51–53. When Robert Brown looked at pollen grains in water through a microscope, he saw them jiggling randomly. Einstein explained this in 1905: invisible water molecules bombard the visible grain from all sides. Statistical imbalances in the bombardment cause the grain to wander.

Practical · 20 min

Use a smoke cell under a microscope (Slide 52). Illuminate from the side. Observe the bright specks of smoke jiggling erratically. Draw the path of a single particle over 1 minute. This is Brownian motion — direct evidence that air is made of fast-moving molecules too small to see.

Materials: Smoke cell kit, microscope, light source, incense stick or smouldering straw

Key point: We are inferring the existence of molecules from the behaviour of visible particles. This is the reductionist method: explain macro phenomena through micro mechanisms.

Lesson 30 · 45 min State Variables vs Dynamic Variables

The big picture (Slide 50). The microscopic world has dynamic variables: position, velocity, and mass of every individual molecule (xi, vi, mi). The macroscopic world has state variables: P, V, T, n. A thermodynamic state is a set of dynamical states — infinitely many microscopic arrangements give the same macroscopic reading.

Analogy: Knowing the average height of students in a class (state variable) tells you nothing about who's tallest (dynamic variable). Many different class compositions produce the same average.

Discussion: Slide 39 asks: "In defining energy of a system, in what sense does the system have no memory?" Answer: the state variables (P, V, T) don't encode how the system got there — only where it is now. This is what "state function" means.

micro vs macro reductionism
Extension: degrees of freedom
A mole of gas has ~6 × 10²³ molecules, each with 3 position and 3 velocity components — that's ~3.6 × 10²⁴ dynamic variables. Yet we describe the whole system with just 3 state variables (P, V, T). This compression from 10²⁴ to 3 is the miracle of thermodynamics — and the origin of entropy.
Lesson 31 · 45 min The Zeroth and First Laws

The Zeroth Law (Slide 38): If A is in thermal equilibrium with B, and B with C, then A is with C. Temperature is transitive. This seems trivially obvious — but it's the logical foundation that makes temperature a well-defined, measurable quantity. Without it, thermometers don't work.

Demo · 10 min

Three beakers: hot water (A), lukewarm water (B), cold water (C). Put a thermometer in B — read temperature. Now put thermometers in A and C. Wait for all to equilibrate. Verify: TA = TB = TC. Temperature equalises through heat flow (Slide 37): Q flows down the temperature gradient.

Materials: Three beakers, thermometers, hot and cold water

The First Law (Slide 39): Energy is conserved. ΔU = Q − W. The internal energy of a system changes by the heat added minus the work done by the system. This is Joule's insight (Week 5) expressed as a law.

The "hidden energy" question: Mechanical energy can hide as heat (random molecular KE). But momentum can't hide the same way — the sum of random velocity vectors is zero. This is why energy conservation works differently from momentum conservation in thermodynamics.

Lesson 32 · 45 min Maxwell's Demon & Course Synthesis

The thought experiment (Slide 54): Imagine a demon guarding a door between two gas chambers. It lets fast (red) molecules through to the right and slow (blue) molecules to the left. The right side gets hotter, the left cooler — without any work input. This would violate the Second Law of Thermodynamics.

(Information processing requires energy — the demon must do work to measure and decide. Landauer's principle: erasing one bit of information dissipates at least kT ln 2 of energy.)

The Big Picture

The Reductionist Paradigm

From 10²⁴ Variables to 3

Thermodynamics is the science of forgetting details. A mole of gas has roughly 10²⁴ molecules, each with its own position and velocity. Yet three numbers — pressure, volume, temperature — describe the whole system. The laws of thermodynamics are the rules that govern this compression.

Microscopic (Dynamic)

xi, vi, mi for each molecule
~10²⁴ variables
Newton's laws govern each particle
Time-reversible at the individual level

⟹

Macroscopic (State)

P, V, T, n
3–4 variables
Gas laws & thermodynamic laws
Irreversible (entropy increases)

The pattern → mechanism → law engine we've used all course is exactly how physics was built. Boyle measured PV. Boltzmann explained it with molecules. The First and Second Laws captured what always happens regardless of the details. This movement from description to explanation to universal principle is what makes thermodynamics — born from steam engines and waterfalls — one of the deepest and most enduring branches of physics.
